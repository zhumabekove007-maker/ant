Theories & Research Methods

A theory is "a statement or statements of how and why specific facts are related" (Macionis), or, more fully, a set of interconnected hypotheses offering general explanations for social or natural phenomena (Scupin & DeCorse). The terms theory, perspective, and school of thought are used interchangeably below. Click any theory to expand its premise, strengths, and weaknesses.

How the Discipline Was Born: Culture vs. Civilization

By the 18th century, "civilization" had become a value-laden word: not just an orderly, "civil" society, but an achieved, progressive endpoint of human development — contrasted with "barbarism," and historically equated with the Greco-Roman inheritance of England and France. This idea fed directly into 19th-century unilineal evolutionism (above), and was reinforced by genuine breakthroughs in geology (Charles Lyell's restructuring of the earth's time depth) and biology (Darwin's 1859 theory of natural selection) — achievements that many social theorists borrowed wholesale and applied to society itself.

Social Darwinism & the Comparative Method

  • Transferred biological evolutionary laws onto the socioeconomic sphere, reinforcing the idea that European societies were "naturally" superior.
  • The "comparative" or "taxonomic" method let armchair theorists compare myths, marriage forms, and technology across societies and arrange the most similar-looking ones into one hypothetical evolutionary sequence ending in European civilization.

Boas's Break

  • Franz Boas separated culture from biology: he showed that the skull shapes of children born in the US to immigrant parents differed measurably from their parents' homeland populations — proof that environment and nutrition, not fixed race, shaped such traits.
  • Where 19th-century theorists had biologized culture, Boas effectively reversed it — treating what looked "biological" as culturally and historically produced instead.

Diffusion vs. independent invention: Evolutionists assumed that similar customs appearing in different societies were each independently invented — supposed proof that those societies had reached the same developmental rung. Boas argued instead for diffusion: cultural elements spread between societies and get selectively adopted, modified, or rejected by the receiving culture. For Boas, civilization is never the product of one people's unique genius; the real question is how outside influences get reworked to fit what's already there.

American vs. British schools: This pluralistic, Boasian notion of culture defines the American school of anthropology. The British school developed differently out of Durkheimian sociology, emphasizing social structure, law, and kinship — chiefly through fieldwork in Africa. A useful image for the difference is an orchestra: social structure is the division of labor between sections and the chain of command from the conductor down; culture is closer to the shared musical meaning that makes the sound intelligible as music at all, rather than noise.

The Greek poet C.P. Cavafy's "Waiting for the Barbarians" captures this tension between culture and civilization in narrative form: a city's senators, orators, and emperor spend an entire day in ceremonial anticipation of an arriving barbarian army, only to learn at nightfall that no barbarians are coming after all — leaving the poem to close on the unsettling thought that the threatening outsider had been giving the city's rituals and institutions their purpose all along. — summarized from C.P. Cavafy's poem, as discussed in Seremetakis (2017)

Chapter Three

The Concept of Culture

Culture is the central concept of anthropology — the capacity that separates humans from nonhumans. It is used loosely in everyday speech, but anthropology gives it a precise, technical meaning.

Culture vs. Society

These two terms are easy to blur, so the chapter draws a clean line between them:

Society

The social world itself — its structures, institutions, and organizations — and specifically a group of people who live within a bounded territory and share a common way of life.

Culture

The common way of life shared by that group of people — everything beyond pure biology: production, dress, food, architecture, education, government, law, and more.

Three Common Misconceptions

Anthropologists push back on how "culture" gets used in everyday speech:

Tylor's Classic Definition

"That complex whole which includes knowledge, belief, art, law, morals, customs, and any other capabilities and habits acquired by man as a member of society." — Edward B. Tylor (1832–1917)

The phrase "acquired by man as a member of society" is the load-bearing part of this definition: it separates traits we get from biological heredity from those we get by growing up inside a particular social group. The process of learning a culture's rules and values is called enculturation.

The complementary move is "seeing small." Clifford Geertz's idea of thick description means reading the grand realities of power, change, faith, oppression, work, passion, authority, beauty, violence, love, and prestige in the fine-grained, give-and-take detail of ordinary daily life — deliberately stripping the capital letters off those big abstractions until they're visible in concrete behavior. Michael Wesch makes a related point: culture isn't just the environment around a person, since it's part of them — it's the very thing that lets them notice anything at all. Getting access to that level of detail and nuance is exactly what long-term ethnographic fieldwork, often lasting many months or years, is for.

Race & Ethnicity

Anthropology was once called the "science of race." Since the 1970s, genetic research has dismantled that label: physical anthropologists have shown that biologically distinct human races do not exist. That doesn't mean race "isn't real" — it means race is real as a social and historical concept, not a biological one.

Race: A Discredited Concept in Human Biology

Centuries of repetition turned an inaccurate idea into something that felt like obvious, concrete "truth" — a process anthropologists call reification. Here's why the biology doesn't hold up:

Race as a Social Concept

Race is a socio-historical concept: racial categories and their meaning are given shape by the specific social relations and historical context they're embedded in — a process Michael Omi and Howard Winant called racial formation. In the United States, this took the form of the one-drop rule (also called hypodescent, a term coined by Marvin Harris): any trace of known non-European ancestry was enough to exclude a person from being classified as "white," automatically assigning mixed-heritage people to the less privileged category. Since 1977, the federal government has used five official categories under OMB Directive 15 — White, Black or African American, American Indian or Alaska Native, Asian, and Native Hawaiian or Other Pacific Islander — plus a separate ethnic (not racial) category for Hispanic or Latino.

The 2000 census was the first to let respondents select more than one race, and prominent biracial and multiracial public figures have since pushed back against rigid, discrete categories — though OMB Directive 15's categories remain in use for civil-rights recordkeeping today.

Ethnicity & Ethnic Groups

Race

An attempt — by outsiders or by society at large — to categorize humans based on arbitrary observed physical or biological differences.

Ethnic group

A group that claims a distinct identity for itself, based on shared cultural characteristics (language, religion, dress, customs) and a shared sense of ancestry or peoplehood.

Symbolic ethnicity describes limited, occasional displays of ethnic pride that are mainly for public show rather than daily practice — "Kiss Me, I'm Irish!" buttons, Celtic-cross tattoos, a Virgin of Guadalupe decal — common among descendants of groups several generations removed from active discrimination. Ethnogenesis is the gradual emergence of a new, distinct ethnic identity in response to changing social circumstances, as when dozens of distinct West and Central African ethnic heritages were flattened by slavery and then re-formed, over generations, into a single African American identity.
